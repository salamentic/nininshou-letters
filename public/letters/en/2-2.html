Thanks for your letter. I found the poem you recently let me read to be incredibly interesting. The poetry I’ve studied up until now- regardless of whether it be fixed or free verse, has been written on the basis that poetry can be composed from words alone, but your poems are written in a form that is designed to be accompanied by music. I didn’t think that by any chance, someone would end up sending me their real work, so I was very surprised.  And further, the word “school” appeared a lot in the letters from the other day. A long time ago, I used to be a teacher, so this made me feel a little bit nostalgic.  I’ll let you know in advance, although I may be able to teach you about kanji, grammar, and prose, I do not want to assert any correction on the subject of your poems. So, if you sent me your work in the hopes of some audacious corrections on the subject matter, then I apologise.  Now, it would become a discussion on what we call a matter of philosophical opinion. For example, in the Meiji era, there was this haiku poet named Masaoka Shiki. You might’ve already learnt about him at school . He vehemently criticised what were called “Soushou Haikus.” “Soushou” is a word that’s used to refer to someone who coaches you in what you’d imagine in for example waka poetry, sadou tea ceremony, kadou flower arrangement, that sort of field. Hence, “Soushou Haiku” refers to the poems you compose whilst being coached on form or etiquette by a Soushou teacher.
