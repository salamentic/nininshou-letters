Dear Sensei, The maidenhair trees are slowly gaining their color. It's getting colder. Even now, the letters don't arrive in plain brown envelopes. When I asked my mother about you, she didn't seem to know that I had noticed the envelopes being swapped. Even now, perhaps early on Saturday mornings, she takes the letters you've sent and transfers them into different envelopes—just so I won't see the address, which could be easily traced to a prison. She repacks them into long Western-style envelopes, then late at night, after coming home, slips them back into our mailbox. Because of that, she doesn't even notice the contradiction of receiving mail on a Sunday, when there's normally no delivery. Sensei, I've always wondered why you always send the poems I write back to me. Because you return them, at least I know you're reading them. But why don't you say anything anymore? Why do you send back only the empty envelope? Do you remember when you taught me about butterflies? The story about Zhou's butterfly. They say that human good and evil are two sides of the same coin. So which one are you, Sensei? Were you a bad person who dreams of your better self, or a good person who dreams dreamed of your worse self? If drinking is just you throwing away your reason, then it's the former. But if drinking simply dulls your judgment, then it's the latter, isn't it?
