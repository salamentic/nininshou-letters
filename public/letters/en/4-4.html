The “play” in “play sick” refers to “play” as in “playing a role.” A literal translation would be “to act sick,” so, that’s the reason it’s translated to “feigned illness.” The etymology is an old English word, “Plegan.” I believe, originally, it was a word that referred to fighting, or playing, and other such physical activities. No, perhaps back then there was also a connotation of acting? Since humankind’s social activities and things like theater are inseparable. Thinking about it like this, I think it’s a good poem that has unintentionally, but properly grasped and expressed that ambiguous side of “play.” It’s both a song that plays at feigned illness, and one which is acting as if one has a cold. I hesitated to point this out, but the way you’ve used “miserable” is considered grammatically incorrect. If you have used “to be miserable” there would be no issue. However, since it’s your own original expression, I think it’s fine to leave it as is. If a melody is to be attached, surely the way you’ve used it would sometimes be better. Isn’t it a wonderful aspect of expression to embrace mistakes such as that?
