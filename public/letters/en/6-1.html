Dear Sensei,

Sensei—the weather is nice out today. I’ve been mulling over the theme about “the Sun” you suggested to me—turning it over and over again in my mind. I tried to write something, but I was unable to make anything decent, and I’ve just ended up wasting a lot of time. This is the first time this has ever happened to me. Until now, I’ve just been recycling what I’ve always written about, waiting for whatever words would appear in my head. I’ve come to realize that I had always just been relying on my usual tropes to write about, so I’m feeling awfully defeated. It becomes difficult when you’re assigned a theme by somebody, doesn’t it?

I decided that I needed a change of pace—so I went out for a walk, and there I saw a butterfly that landed in a patch of tall grass. It was on the larger side, with black spots freckled upon its yellow wings. It slowly opened its wings and looked content as it bathed in the sunlight. It let its body shiver oh-so-slightly when the wind rustled the grass. It was then that I suddenly remembered Hagiwara Sakutarou’s poem. I know you’re very well-versed in this kind of thing, so I’m sure that you’ll know it, but it’s the first poem of his “Dream of a Butterfly” poetry collection.


  Within the newly matted room,

  a butterfly spreads its wings.

  It strains to open them—

  White, heavy wings,

  Like sheets of paper.


And so I began to try likening the sunlight to a butterfly. Upon doing so, almost like I never struggled at all before to write it, my pen started moving on its own, and I ended up composing a whole piece overnight.
