Thank you for your reply. Are the words that we use ourselves just an aggregation of borrowed sentences? If that’s so, then I have no hesitation in borrowing their words and their full context too.

Why of course, it’s just like that. From when our ancestors first began putting spoken words into letters, words began to fuse with other languages and eventually, after borrowing so many times over and over again, they began to melt together and settled into the shape we know language to be today. It could even be said that our mother tongue itself is a mishmash of borrowed language. Even so, you must not use that as an escape route. If you prepare excuses, the impact that your writing has will diminish. If words were to be a small grain of sand, the vast lump of sand gathered by humankind over a long time must be their context in which they are shaped. And it is another truth that inside that sand, there’s a tiny piece of amber that at first glance just appears to be a normal grain of just sand. I hope that you’ll be able to understand my analogy. From now on, across an unfathomably vast sea of sand, you must forage for but a single drop of amber. There is strength in intertextual reference. Because it’s like you’re using the amber that somebody else has already set out to find. However, that is only ever a tool.
