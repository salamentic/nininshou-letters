In fact, it’s said that during the Edo period, there was a profession called a “Haikai Soushou” who taught Haikai (collective name for haiku poetry) in exchange for money. That kind of systemic teacher-student Haiku culture was widespread. Then, Masaoka Shiki wrote “The Soushou’s Haiku are like sandboxes- child’s play.”  I believe that this is indeed the case. To learn from somebody, to edit your work to match your teacher’s values as you write isn’t art in of itself, it’s a performance art. A Keiko (training/practice) method such as the one used to learn Ikebana wouldn’t suit a craft such as poetry.  But of course, just simply labelling “performance art” as an evil would be overwhelmingly brash, shallow, and superficial. I may have previously written in the disagreeable Keiko-style, but I still believe that every expression, whether refined or coarse, should be viewed as equal. But, from my personal standpoint, I don’t think that I want to correct anyone’s expressions. This is because that would be like dying their work with my own values and way of writing. So, I don’t want to turn your poetry- that only you can write- into a collaboration between you and I. But of course, if you’d like to discuss methods of expression, then I think I can do that. If you so desire, I can teach you several ways of manipulating languages too. Depending on how you get encouraged by this exchange of letters with me, there’s the possibility that new ideas will flash into your head. Take it easy and send me anything anytime.
