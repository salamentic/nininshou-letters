So when the first envelope arrived after our correspondence began, my mother looked at it and, on impulse, checked what was inside. After that, she threw away the brown envelope with the prison address on it, replaced it with a long Western-style envelope from our house, and dropped it back into our mailbox. That, apparently, was how everything began. If she stopped switching the envelopes, I might write in a letter that the envelope had suddenly changed. And she didn't know what to do with an envelope she had already opened. Honestly, I think she's a bit foolish. My mother opens envelopes by cutting them with scissors. If she wanted to peek secretly, she could have peeled them open with her fingers and glued them shut again. She might have gotten away with it a little better. My mother simply apologized to me. She asked me to believe at least one thing: that she never checked the contents of my poems. She said she felt that was something she must never do. Because you told her that a poem is an act of showing one's true inner self. You told her repeatedly that she should wait until I myself chose to reveal what I was expressing and writing. That had been the promise the two of you made when you first began the correspondence. Now I also understand why that sentence “You're reading this now, aren't you?” hadn't been removed. Both the first time, and the second time when I looked up your address, I had taken the letter from the mailbox myself early in the morning.
