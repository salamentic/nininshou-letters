Dear Sensei, The neighborhood around my house has completely turned to autumn colors. The wind is gradually growing colder, and you can tell that winter is just around the corner. But the clouds are still patchy, and the sunlight is warm. It feels like swimming through waves. Since I started going to school again, I've made some friends. Only a few, really, but even when the evening chime rings, I don't feel that tightness in my chest very often anymore. I keep rereading the letters you sent me. How have you been, Sensei? After that, my mother and I talked, just the two of us. It was different from the one time before when I tried to get her to tell me about you. It might have been the first time in years that we sat face to face and talked for that long. Sensei, you and my mother have been meeting regularly, haven't you? Did she talk about me when she saw you? One day, my mother told me that she had asked you to correspond with her son. She said, “My son writes poetry. His health is fragile, and he hasn't been going to school because he can't quite fit in with the people around him. Please teach my son about literature.” She asked you that because you had once held a teaching position, teaching literature. Up to this point, the two of you understood the situation the same way. But beyond that, things diverged. You naturally believed that I knew you were an inmate serving a sentence. But my mother couldn't decide, right until the end, whether she should tell me that or not.
