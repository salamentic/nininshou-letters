Based on the explanation in the book, a loose translation would be: “I know the vast sea, mere water would not be enough for me. I cannot imagine any clouds other than those of Mount Wu.” This was a love poem. Yuan Zhen had lost his wife, and upon having learned that, my perspective of his poem changed completely. The “sea” and the “cloud” are metaphors referring to the moments he shared with his wife; he tells that deceased person that nothing is worth more than the time he spent with her. Sensei, what you taught me was a fragment of a poem like this. By just knowing the context, I can see how those dozens of characters possess such vivid colors. Just as I thought, knowledge is something amazing. I'm not sure how to put this kind of thing into words. It is absolutely unmistakable. It's a beautiful light that appears within anyone, depending on their effort. After that, I thought about writing the poem “Snake.” In this poem, I borrowed Yuan Zhen's poem and compared knowledge to a snake. You might think there's no connection between them. But even so, I feel that snakes and the desire to know are two things that are related. Perhaps ever since I saw Milton's “Paradise Lost” in my mother's study room. In the Bible, the snake tempts humans to eat the fruit of wisdom. It is as if the snake itself transforms into the desire for knowledge.
