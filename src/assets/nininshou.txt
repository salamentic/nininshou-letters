In fact, back in the Edo period there was an occupation known as a “Haikai master” who taught Haikai* for pay, in this manner. This form of student and teacher based Haiku was quite widespread as you can probably tell. To this, Shiki said: 


“Haiku written under a master is a miniature garden surrounded by walls, closed and artificial.” 


I feel the same way.
  
Writing while being taught by someone and then adjusting it to your teacher’s worldview is not art, it’s a guided performance. The Ikebana way of creation under supervision is just not feasible with poetry. 
  
Of course, I’m not saying guided performances are meaningless, that would be very shallow of me. While guided writing does not fit my ideology, no matter how highbrow or lowbrow it is, it’s still of equal value. 
That said, I personally never want to meddle with how someone expresses themselves. In doing so I would impose my worldview and style on another person, making their work partly mine. I do not want you to write something in collaboration with me, I want you to write your own poems. That said, I am happy to give you advice on how to express yourself. If you so wish, I can even teach you new words or concepts.

※ Regarding１
The Chinese language has simplified and traditional characters. While traditional characters were used for a very long time, as the ‘traditional’ suggests, simplified characters were introduced to make writing and reading a little easier.
What you’ve written is the simplified form of the expressions. It goes without saying that Yuan Zhens’ original writing was written in traditional script, so the more accurate way to write this is:


曾經滄海難為水  
除卻巫山不是雲


※ Regarding 2
This a simple metaphor for the thirst for knowledge. While China generally relates the Serpent to the concept of longevity, it was also an animal that represented wisdom. In their ancient literature, you’ll find a lot of pieces that represent the serpent as a symbol for immortality or revival. Snakes shed their skin, don’t they? Perhaps the people from back then looked at this as the snake coming back to life, and considered it to be mystic. Since things that live long are sure to possess wisdom, the snake became associated with the virtue of wisdom. In reality, the process of shedding its skin is just another biological function that’s part of the snake aging.

You looked into “Thoughts of Separation”, I see. I appreciate that you took my recommendation. Strictly speaking, this is a seven-character quatrain belonging to a set of five poems titled “Five Poems on Parting Thoughts”. This particular piece is the fourth in that series. The poem itself references the philosopher Mencius:


孟子日、孔子登東山而小魯、登太山而小天下 
故觀於海者難為水、遊於聖人之門者難為言


<He puts the JP version here as well. Maybe someone can find the Pinyin? I couldn’t>


If we translate the second half:


To someone who has seen the sea, a river is nothing but a small body of water.
To someone who has studied under a sage, other’s views are nothing but trivial.


Using the same rhetoric that Mencius used when he looked down on the world after descending Mt. Wu, Yuan Zhen is singing about his love for his late-wife. When he says “Clouds of Mt. Wu” he is referencing the “clouds and rain of Mt.Wu” from the Gaotang Fu. In doing so, he is alluding to a man and woman uniting in love within their dreams. That said, having read your poem about the Serpent, I can tell that you already understand this, so I won’t prattle on.
